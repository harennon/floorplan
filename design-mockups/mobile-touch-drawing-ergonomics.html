Draw & snap with a finger

The editor's interaction model is mouse-first: it commits a wall vertex on pointer up and relies on hover to preview the snap under the cursor. A finger has no hover, and it occludes the very point it is trying to place. These mockups explore how touch drawing, moving, and pinch/pan should feel — without regressing desktop.

The shared ergonomics (touch-sized targets, gesture disambiguation, no page scroll/zoom) are identical in both directions. What differs is how a snapped vertex is committed under a fingertip. Pick a direction for that; the rest is settled.

A

Direction A — Offset loupe (touch-and-drag, lift to place)

drag · lift = commit

Touch down anywhere and a crosshair floats above your fingertip showing the resolved snap point, so your finger never hides it. Drag to fine-tune; the live snap tag follows. Lift to commit that vertex and continue the chain. This mirrors the current down→drag→up flow — no new gesture to learn.

  • 1
    Loupe offset. The crosshair renders a fixed screen offset above the contact point so the fingertip never occludes the snap. Reuses the existing onHover(sx, sy-OFFSET) path with an offset applied while a touch pointer is active.
  • 2
    Commit on lift. Lifting the finger fires the existing onClick and starts the next segment — no extra tap. Matches today's _drawPending → onClick logic exactly; only the snap coordinate is offset.
  • 3
    Finish the chain. The rail's checkmark (or tapping the start vertex to close) ends the chain, same as Enter/Esc on desktop.
B

Direction B — Aim-then-confirm crosshair

drag to aim · tap ⊕ = commit

A persistent crosshair lives at screen centre (or wherever you last dragged it). Dragging anywhere nudges it; the snap resolves at the crosshair. A thumb-reachable ⊕ Add point button commits the vertex — placement is decoupled from the touch, so precise aiming and committing never fight. Great one-handed; two taps per point is the tradeoff.

  • 1
    Reticle is state, not touch. One-finger drag moves the reticle (calls onHover at the reticle position); the finger need not be on the target. Adds a persistent reticle position to wallTool; touch drags update it instead of committing.
  • 2
    Explicit commit. ⊕ calls the existing onClick(reticleX, reticleY). Never accidental — panning or resting a palm can't drop a point. Clean gesture separation at the cost of a second tap per vertex.
  • 3
    Undo / Finish in reach. The thumb bar keeps chain controls where a one-handed grip can hit them, replacing keyboard Backspace/Enter.
✦

Shared — touch-sized handles, inspector & furniture dock

identical in A & B

Selection, move, resize, rotate, and furniture placement are the same regardless of the drawing direction chosen. Hit targets grow to ≥44 px on touch (up from the 12/14 px used for mouse) with an invisible padded hit ring, and the dock becomes a swipeable bottom sheet.

  • 1
    ≥44 px hit targets. Resize/rotate handles render at 28 px with an 8 px invisible pad → 44 px touch target. Bump MIN_HIT_PX / ROTATE_HIT_R when a coarse pointer is detected (matchMedia('(pointer: coarse)')).
  • 2
    One-finger drag = move. Unchanged onSelectDown → onSelectMove → onSelectUp path; a coarse pointer just gets a larger pick tolerance.
  • 3
    Dock as bottom sheet. The furniture dock docks to the bottom edge (swipe to reveal more), keeping the drawing surface clear and away from the OS edge-swipe zones.
⇢

Gesture disambiguation (both directions)

draw vs pan vs pinch
☝
1 finger
Draw mode → place / aim vertex. Select mode → move symbol, else pan the canvas.
✌
2 fingers
Always pinch-zoom + pan together. A second finger cancels any in-flight draw/move (no stray vertex).
⏱
threshold
Move under ~10 px within tap-time = a tap; beyond = a drag. Larger than the 6 px mouse threshold to forgive finger jitter.
🚫
no page scroll
touch-action:none on the stage + overscroll-behavior:none already present — the page never scrolls or pinch-zooms while drawing.

This logic already exists in interactions.js (_pointers map, pinch seeding, threshold). The work is tuning thresholds for touch, applying the crosshair offset (A) or reticle state (B), and detecting coarse pointers to grow hit targets — no change to the desktop mouse path.

Recommendation

Direction A (Offset loupe) is the recommended default. It keeps the exact desktop commit model (down → drag → lift), needs the least new state, and matches the "sketch in two minutes" principle — one gesture per point, nothing to learn. The finger-occlusion problem is solved purely by offsetting the snap preview above the contact point.

Direction B (Aim-then-confirm) is stronger for precision one-handed work and eliminates any chance of an accidental vertex, but costs a second tap per point and adds persistent reticle state. Offer it as a fallback if A's lift-to-commit tests poorly on real devices.

Shared ergonomics (handles, dock, gesture disambiguation) ship regardless of the choice. Reply with Frontend decision: A or B (or "A with B as opt-in").
